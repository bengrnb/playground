Parkit

Outcome

Created a prototype of a mobile application that would allow users to list and rent residential parking spaces.

Client

Unsolicited tool for Ann Arbor residents

Type

Product Development, UX Research, UX Design

Date

September - December 2022

Roles

UX Researcher, UX Designer, Product Developer

  • Overview

  • Ann Arbor parking is currently much more expensive and difficult to find than it should be. Especially in croweded areas and during popular events such as football games, residents can spend a lot of time driving around looking for parking spaces; sometimes not being able to access emenities they knew would be available. Some examples of other parking services exist, however their service and care is not as reliable and optimal for customers. When residents do find parking, the cost of a space is high and there are no affordable alternatives. Parking should be consistent and accommodate everyone. There needs to be a fair and equitable change in finding parking options that are affordable and realistic to get a hold of.

  • Our primary goal was to improve the parking situation of Ann Arbor by addressing its problems. We were interested in creating a mutually beneficial exchange between residential parking space owners and potential renters to alleviate some of the challenges with parking in Ann Arbor.

  • Project Execution

  • We conducted research and design. Research: Secondary research, interviews, survey, competitive analysis, stakeholder analysis, personas, demo

    • "When looking for a study space, what are the biggest challenges you face?"

    • "When do you look for study spaces the most often (e.g., in-between classes, before classes)?"

    • "What makes a study space a "best-for-you" space?"

    • "What is your favorite place to study on campus? Why?"

    • "What is your least favorite place to study on campus, but where you still study sometimes? Why is it your least favorite? Why do you end up there?"

    • "Lead me through the process you go through when you try to find a place to study or work? How does the process feel?"

    • "How does a "bad"/"good" study space impact you (e.g., concentration, mood, productivity)?"

  • Findings (User Interviews)

  • I found that there are a lot of study spaces on campus, but no central service to sort through and view all of the spaces, which makes it difficult for students to discover new spaces. Additionally, there is very little informaiton provided about study spaces, and students have specific preferences for what constitutes a "good" space for them.

  • Students want to be able to find spaces quickly and many preferred to find spaces that were close by, so that they could access them between classes and travel to them on foot. The needs students had for study spaces differed as well, depending on personal preferences and the tasks they needed to complete (e.g. background noise vs silent, group work vs individual work).

  • Quotes from Student interviews included:

    • "favorite spot?...the [room] is very bright and you can see outside."

    • "I don't have a preferred spot, but I usually opt with whatever is closest to me."

    • "[Study spaces at umich.edu] lists some spaces but it doesn't tell where they are. It's just some room, in some building, and the amount is very limited."

    • "...I feel extreme frustration [trying to find a spot]... there's been many times where I've walked through the whole building and there's no spaces, and then I walk to another building, and there's no spaces. It's very frustrating because then you've wasted a bunch of time!"

    • "If I'm already stressed out, and need to get a bunch of work done [finding a study space] can be kind of stressful, especially when I want a certain envrionment. When it's around exams, or certain hours of the week day, it can be really busy and people are talking and its hard to focus in certain spaces which can be kind of stressful."

    • I think [a good study space] really helps me get my work done. When I can find perfect ideal environment, I can just sit there for like five hours and grind and get so much done. I do think it really impacts how I feel about my learning and how much I can get done.

  • Competitive Analysis

  • In order to optimize the development of the U of M Spaces tool, I looked at what my main competitor - Study Space at U-M, as well as 4 other university based space search tools/systems were offering, to see what I could learn from their strengths and weaknesses.

  • The tools all offered a way to locate spaces (some with very limited options), with some ability to reserve spaces, but their reservation functions were confucing to use and often produced misleading results. Some tools included very limited descriptions of amenities in the rooms (e.g., printers) and a few offered the option to filter spaces based on limited amenity offerings. None of the tools offered students the option to upload their own pictures of spaces, to review the spaces or suggest spaces. Finally, none of the competitors had all of the elements student users reported that they needed/wanted.

  • Mockup

  • Combining the results of the background and user research and the competitive analysis, I identified key elements which I deemed the most important to address the problem. I included these key elements in the creation of a low, medium and then high fidelity prototype, using Figma. Important elements include:

    • Searching for a study space

    • Discovering new study spaces

    • Favoriting and reserving study spaces

    • Reviewing a study space, and adding a picture or description

    • Submitting a request to add a new space to the system

  • Outcomes and Impact

  • If implemented, the tool will help students at U of M easily and efficiently locate and possibly reserve study spaces which are nearby and match their needs for certain features and resources, securing a more optimal study space for them.

  • The tool will have a positive social impact on the U of M campus in 3 ways:

    • It will promote social engagement with the campus community by encouraging students to contribute to the growth of the site to help one another have a better experience finding best-for-them study spaces.

    • As the tool reduces frustration and stress for already stressed students when they are looking for spaces, and helps people do their best academically by helping them identify optimal study spaces, the tool will promote mental health and academic success.

    • The tool will promote equity and support students' diverse needs; the tool makes it easy for all students to access the same information, quickly and easily. In addition, I hope that having access to rich information will be of special help to students with disabilities who may need extra help identifying spaces that are a good fit for their needs.
